## Lekin savol AI haqida emas edi

Savol ishonch haqida edi. Yoki aniqrog'i — vaqt haqida.

Kompaniya rahbari sotishni bilishi mumkin. Lekin kuniga 8 soat qo'ng'iroq qilib o'tirsa, kompaniyani kim boshqaradi? Restoran egasi ovqat pishira oladi. Lekin oshxonada turib qolsa, biznesni kim rivojlantiradi?

AI ham xuddi shunday joyga tushadi — faqat boshqa burchakdan. AI vaqtni tejaydi, lekin qarorni o'zi qabul qilmaydi. Va aynan shu joyda mutaxassis kerak bo'lib qoladi: AI *nima qilish kerakligini* aytmaydi, u faqat *aytilganini* qiladi.

## SEO'da bu qanday ko'rinadi

Keling, aniq misolga o'taman.

ChatGPT'dan "menga meta description yoz" desangiz — yozadi. 10 soniyada, chiroyli, grammatik xato yo'q. Ajoyib.

Lekin ChatGPT bilmaydigan narsalar bor:

- Sizning saytingiz Search Console'da qaysi so'rov bo'yicha 15-o'rinda turgani
- Raqobatchilaringiz haqiqatda qanday kontent bilan g'olib chiqayotgani
- Toshkentdagi mijozingiz "eng yaxshi" so'zini emas, "yaqin" so'zini qidirishi
- Sizning avvalgi 5 ta postingiz allaqachon qaysi mavzuni yopib qo'ygani

Bularning barchasi — kontekst. AI'da kontekst yo'q, faqat siz (yoki mutaxassisingiz) unga bergan ma'lumot bor. Kontekstsiz yozilgan meta description — chiroyli, lekin ishlamaydi. Chunki u *hamma uchun* yozilgan, *sizning mijozingiz uchun* emas.

Why do people still buy services from developers even though AI exists?

Why does a CEO who knows how to sell still hire a sales team?

Why does a restaurant owner who knows how to cook still hire a chef?

Why does a business owner who understands advertising still hire a marketing specialist?

After all, they could do all of it themselves.

So why did the moment AI showed up, everyone suddenly started asking: "Are developers not going to be needed anymore?"

I think you already got a clear answer to your question — through questions.

## But the question was never about AI

It was about trust. Or more precisely — about time.

A CEO might know how to sell. But if they spend 8 hours a day on sales calls, who runs the company? A restaurant owner can cook. But if they're stuck in the kitchen, who grows the business?

AI lands in the same spot, just from a different angle. AI saves time, but it doesn't make the decision itself. And that's exactly where a specialist is still needed: AI doesn't tell you *what* to do — it only does *what it's told*.

## What that looks like in SEO

Let's get concrete.

Ask ChatGPT to "write me a meta description" — it will. In 10 seconds, polished, no grammar mistakes. Great.

But here's what ChatGPT doesn't know:

- Which query your site currently ranks 15th for in Search Console
- What content your competitors are actually winning with
- That your Tashkent customer searches "nearby," not "best"
- Which topic your last 5 posts already covered

All of that is context. AI has none of it — only what you (or your specialist) feed it. A meta description written without context is polished but doesn't work. Because it was written *for everyone*, not *for your customer*.

One small observation: ask ChatGPT for "an SEO strategy" and it gives a Silicon Valley SaaS company and a small shop in the old bazaar nearly the same answer — just swapping names. Because AI knows the "generic correct average answer." Your specific situation — it doesn't.

## What belongs to AI, what belongs to a human

| Task | Who does it |
|---|---|
| Meta tags, draft copy | AI — fast and good |
| Keyword list generation | AI — in minutes |
| Listing technical errors | AI — efficient |
| Deciding what to tackle first | Specialist |
| Interpreting Search Console data | Specialist |
| Adapting to a specific market | Specialist |
| Taking responsibility when it doesn't work | Specialist |

AI is a tool. A hammer can be a great hammer, but it doesn't decide which nail goes where.

## So AI doesn't eliminate the specialist — it changes the job

I use AI every day myself — to produce fast drafts, to review data faster. It saves time. But saved time doesn't disappear — it goes into strategy, deeper analysis, understanding the customer's exact situation.

I worked the same way at Darslinker: AI produced the first content drafts quickly, but which topic to write first, which keyword to prioritize, how to read the results — those were all manual decisions, made from data.

AI existing doesn't mean the specialist is no longer needed. If anything, the gap widens between a specialist who uses AI well and one who doesn't. One turns a week of work into a day and spends the rest on strategy. The other just stays slow.

## In closing

AI writes code, generates text, produces reports. But it doesn't make decisions in your place, doesn't take responsibility, and doesn't know your specific market.

Just like a CEO who can sell still hires a salesperson, and a cook still hires a chef — AI existing doesn't remove the need for an SEO specialist. It just changes how the specialist works: no longer with pen and paper, but with powerful tools, while thinking the same way.
